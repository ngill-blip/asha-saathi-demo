CbHCM Program · ASHA TPT · Vendor Brief

A digital refresher tool to substantiate our Training-of-Trainers.

TPT knowledge reaches frontline ASHAs through a cascade of in-person trainings — master trainers, to district and block trainers, to a single classroom session. That cascade is fragile, decays fast, and we have no way to prove it landed. This is the case for a lightweight, voice-first WhatsApp refresher that keeps the training alive between cases — and turns every lesson into evidence.

The problem01

A one-time cascade can't carry a year of fieldwork.

Every hop of the Training-of-Trainers loses fidelity, and the final ASHA session happens once. With infrequent TPT casework between visits, skills go unused and erode — and the program has no signal on what survived.
↓

Knowledge decays

Without reinforcement, most of a single training is forgotten within weeks. Infrequent casework means skills go unused and erode further.

≈

Cascade drift

Each layer of the ToT paraphrases the last. By the time content reaches ASHAs, key messages and counselling structure are diluted or inconsistent.

?

No substantiation

We can confirm a training happened (attendance), but never that it worked. No read on retention, gaps, or field confidence.

From our own field prototyping · Feb 2026

This isn't hypothetical. The findings flagged it directly: "ASHAs have low retention of training, and there is a time gap between training and case allotment." CHOs also reported that a single counselling session is rarely enough — families need repeated, consistent contact before they test or start TPT, and ASHAs were using no supporting artefact at all.

The solution02

A voice-first WhatsApp refresher — one short lesson at a time.

Built for a low-literacy audience on variable rural connectivity: 8–10 minute lessons (5–7 min content, 3 min activity), every lesson grounded in a real step of the TPT pathway, following one repeatable structure.
Open

Anchor

30-sec voice note framing a real field moment.

Deliver

Core lesson

3–5 min warm Hindi audio + a standalone image cue.

Check

Scenario MCQ

"What would you do if…" applied to the pathway.

Q&A window

Voice/text question routed to CHO; common ones auto-answered.

Reinforce

Spaced recap

A revision MCQ a few days later locks it in.

Design rule

The ASHA should be able to complete a lesson by listening alone. Text is a label, not the content. Images must stand on their own as field memory cues she can even show to households.

How it gets built03

Built on WhatsApp features ASHAs already use.

No new app to learn, no smartphone hurdle. Basic, known WhatsApp Business features are creatively adapted to deliver, check, and reinforce training — running on the WhatsApp Business platform (e.g. an open-source layer like Glific on the WhatsApp Business API).
Reach & familiarity

ASHAs already live on WhatsApp daily — zero onboarding friction, no separate login or device.

Voice-first & multi-modal

Voice notes, images and short video carry the lesson for a low-literacy audience — listening is enough.

Low-data & resilient

Bite-sized messages work on rural bandwidth and basic smartphones; content is consumed at the ASHA's pace.

How often, and what04

Little and often beats long and rare.

We're not guessing at the cadence. Comparable WhatsApp/SMS health programs — and the learning science — converge on a few short, single-concept messages per week, sustained across the relevant window. Our framework's recommended weekly lesson sits right in that band.
ProgramCadenceWhat we take from it
ASHA Saathi (proposed) 1 / week 8–10 min voice-first lesson weekly (fortnightly minimum), each tied to a TPT pathway step, with a spaced recap a few days later.
Noora Health — mobile care companion ~3–4 / week 25 messages incl. 7 videos over 50 days, mirroring the in-person curriculum; concise, spaced, two-way.1
MomConnect — South Africa (Turn.io / Praekelt) 2–3 / week Stage-based messages, ~150 in total, from pregnancy registration through the child's first birthday.2
Glific / CLAP — Key Education Foundation weekly nudges Field lesson for low-literacy parents: short, focused messages outperformed lengthy text or video.3
Learning science — the spacing effect spaced Distributed practice can roughly double long-term retention versus massed (crammed) study.4
